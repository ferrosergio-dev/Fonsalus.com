Accounting Basics in Estonia

2026-03-13 Category: Accounting

Important: This article provides a general overview of the Accounting Act and its amendments. It does not constitute legal or professional advice. Each business situation is unique, and we recommend consulting with our specialists to ensure your accounting practices are fully compliant with the latest regulations.

The Estonian Accounting Act has undergone several significant amendments in recent years to align with European Union directives and meet the needs of the modern business environment. As of 2026, several updates concerning report formats, digital invoicing, and sustainability reporting have come into effect. This article provides an overview of the fundamental principles of Estonian accounting, based on the Accounting Act (Raamatupidamise seadus – RPS) and its latest version (adopted 20.11.2002, last amendment effective 17.01.2025).

1. Accounting Entity and General Principles

An accounting entity is any legal or natural person operating in Estonia who must document their economic activities. According to § 2 of the Act, this includes all private and public legal entities registered in Estonia, sole proprietors, and branches of foreign companies.

The basic requirements for organizing accounting (§ 4) are clear:

  • All business transactions must be documented and recorded.
  • Accounting is generally kept on an accrual basis (§ 5), meaning transactions are recorded when they occur, regardless of cash flow (an exception is made for sole proprietors, who may use cash-based accounting).
  • The accounting must ensure the provision of up-to-date, relevant, objective, and comparable information.

2. Annual Report in 2026

The annual report reflects a company's performance. Its standard length is 12 months, but it can be shorter or longer (max 18 months) in the first or last year of operation.

Composition of the Report

Generally, the annual report consists of the financial statements and the management report. However, there are important distinctions based on the company's size:

  • Micro-entity (total assets under €450,000, revenue under €900,000, and fewer than 10 employees): May prepare an abridged annual report, containing only a balance sheet and a profit and loss statement (the management report is optional).
  • Small entity (total assets under €7.5 million, revenue under €15 million, and fewer than 50 employees): Prepares an abridged report but must include significant notes.
  • Medium-sized and large entities: Must prepare a full report, including a cash flow statement and a statement of changes in equity.

Important! In 2026, new increased thresholds are in effect (e.g., the asset threshold for a small entity has risen to €7.5 million). This means many companies that previously qualified as small entities can now maintain this status.

3. New Regulation: Sustainability Reporting (CSR)

One of the most significant changes included in the amendment from early 2025, affecting 2026 reporting, is the regulation for the sustainability report (part of the management report).

Good news! Following an initiative by the European Commission and amendments to the Estonian Accounting Act, the deadline for submitting the sustainability report has been postponed by two years.

  • Large entities (including those with over 500 employees) were initially required to start in 2026, but the first mandatory reporting period is now 2028 (submitted in 2029).
  • SMEs that are public-interest entities will start in 2029 (submitted in 2030).

Therefore, in 2026, most Estonian companies are not yet required to prepare a sustainability report – except for those who have voluntarily started early.

4. Digital Accounting and e-Invoices

Estonia is moving rapidly towards digital accounting. A significant change concerning e-invoices came into effect on July 1, 2025:

  • Businesses can register themselves in the commercial register as recipients of e-invoices.
  • If a company registers, they can require their suppliers to submit e-invoices.
  • By default, the European e-invoicing standard EN 16931 is used, unless the parties agree otherwise.
  • This applies to both the B2G (public procurement) and B2B sectors. While e-invoicing is not mandatory in B2B, it is becoming the market standard.

This means that by 2026, every entrepreneur must be capable of at least receiving machine-readable invoices if their partner demands it.

5. Source Documents and Record Keeping

The law has clarified requirements for source documents:

  • Source documents must be machine-processable (§ 7). This means data exchange should use a standardized data description (e.g., XML format) so that information systems can process the data automatically.
  • Documents (source documents, registers, reports) must be retained for 7 years (§ 12) from the end of the financial year in which the document was recorded.

6. Internal Accounting Rules

All accounting entities, except micro-entities, must establish internal accounting rules (§ 11). This document must describe the entire accounting process – from the chart of accounts and document workflow to asset inventory and report preparation. It is a crucial document for both tax audits and ensuring continuity within the company.

7. Estonian Financial Reporting Standard (EFRS) and New Guidelines

The Estonian Financial Reporting Standard (EFRS) is based on international standards (IFRS for SMEs). In 2025, the IASB adopted the updated consolidated text of the IFRS for SMEs. Consequently, updates to the Estonian Accounting Committee Guidelines (RTJ) are expected in 2026, likely coming into effect from the beginning of 2027 (for reporting periods starting in 2027 or later).

Key planned changes to prepare for include:

  • Sale of goods with a right of return (affects e-commerce): Revenue is recognized only for the portion not expected to be returned. Returnable goods are recorded on the balance sheet as a "return asset".
  • Business combinations: Acquisition-related costs (legal, consulting fees) are no longer added to the acquisition cost but are expensed immediately.
  • Contingent consideration: Subsequent changes in contingent consideration are recognized in the profit or loss statement, rather than as an adjustment to goodwill.

Summary

In 2026, Estonian accounting regulations are stable but technologically more demanding. Although the deadline for sustainability reporting has been postponed, companies must be prepared for digitally functional accounting and the receipt of e-invoices. It is also wise to familiarize yourself with the upcoming RTJ changes now to ensure smooth reporting in 2027.
