Payroll Calculation in 2026

2026-03-13 Category: Payroll

Important: This article is for informational purposes and describes tax calculation rules according to Estonian legislation. Each business situation is unique — we recommend consulting with our specialists to ensure your payroll processes are fully compliant.

Why Payroll Calculation Matters in 2026

Correct payroll calculation is the foundation of a successful employment relationship and a guarantee of avoiding problems with the tax authority. In 2026, several well-established taxation principles continue to apply in Estonia, but an important new element is being added — the security tax. In this article, we will explain how to correctly calculate wages in 2026, which taxes and from whom to withhold, and what benefits employees can use.

The article is based on the latest versions of the laws in force in 2026, including the new Security Tax Act that came into effect on January 1, 2026.

2026 Update: All employers must now withhold an additional 2% security tax from employee gross wages. This tax is calculated on the full gross amount before any deductions (including tax-exempt income).

1. Initial Data: Minimum Wage and Tax Rates for 2026

Before starting calculations, we need to know the basic parameters set by the state for 2026.

Minimum Wage

As of January 1, 2025 (valid for 2026 calculations):

  • Monthly minimum wage: €886 (for full-time employment)
  • Hourly minimum wage: €5.31

Source: Government Regulation No. 87 of 19.12.2024

2. Important Innovation for 2026: The Security Tax

The Security Tax Act entered into force on January 1, 2026. This tax applies to both the income of natural persons and the profits of companies.

Key Points for Payroll:

  • Who pays: The tax is withheld from the employee's gross income.
  • Rate: 2% of the employee's gross salary.
  • Object of taxation: The tax is calculated on the full gross salary — no deductions allowed (Security Tax Act § 10(3)). This means the tax-exempt amount and funded pension contributions cannot be subtracted before calculating this tax.

Important: The security tax is calculated on the entire gross salary, even if the employee's income is below the tax-exempt threshold. For example, an employee earning €700/month pays €14 in security tax, even though they pay no income tax.

3. Payroll Calculation Formula (Gross → Net) including Security Tax

In 2026, the net salary (take-home pay) is calculated using the following formula:

Net = Gross – Income Tax – Unemployment Insurance (employee) – Security Tax – Funded Pension (employee)

Step-by-Step Calculation:

  1. Employee's Unemployment Insurance: Gross × 1.6%
  2. Funded Pension (II pillar): Gross × employee's chosen rate (2%, 4%, or 6%)
  3. Security Tax: Gross × 2%
  4. Income Tax Base: Gross – Unemployment – Funded Pension – Tax-Exempt Amount
  5. Income Tax: Income Tax Base × 22%
  6. Net Salary: Gross – All deductions

3.1. Tax-Exempt Income in 2026

An important change applies from 2026 (Income Tax Act § 23 and § 235):

  • Persons under 65 (before retirement age): Fixed tax-exempt amount of €700 per month
  • Persons of retirement age: Fixed tax-exempt amount of €776 per month

The previous complex formula with income-dependent reductions has been abolished.

4. Employer's Taxes and Payments (added to gross salary)

The employer must calculate and pay taxes that are added on top of the employee's gross salary. The security tax does not increase the employer's direct costs — it is only withheld from the employee and forwarded to the state.

  • Social Tax: Gross × 33%
  • Employer's Unemployment Insurance: Gross × 0.8%

4.1. Example: Total Employer Cost

Continuing with Example 1 (young specialist, €1800 gross):

Gross Salary:€1,800.00
Social Tax (33%):€594.00
Employer Unemployment (0.8%):€14.40
Total Employer Cost:€2,408.40

Note: The security tax (€36) and income tax are withheld from the employee's gross salary and do not directly increase employer costs. However, the employer is responsible for correctly calculating, withholding, and transferring these amounts to the Tax and Customs Board.

5. Special Cases and Exemptions

5.1. Employees with Reduced Work Ability

Employers can claim a refund of part of the paid social tax for employees with partial or no work ability (Social Tax Act § 6). This is an important state support measure for promoting employment.

5.2. Exemption from Funded Pension

The following employees are exempt from II pillar contributions:

  • Employees of retirement age
  • Employees with established partial or no work ability
  • Employees who have submitted an application to suspend payments

Employers must track these exemptions and not deduct funded pension from exempt employees' wages.

5.3. Security Tax Exemptions?

The Security Tax Act does not provide standard deductions (like tax-exempt income). However, the tax is not withheld from certain types of income that are not subject to income tax at all. But if the income is taxable, the security tax applies to its full amount.

6. Employer Responsibilities: Deadlines and Declarations

6.1. Payment Deadlines

All taxes and contributions must be transferred to the Tax and Customs Board's bank account by the 10th day of the month following the payment month. This includes:

  • Income tax
  • Social tax
  • Unemployment insurance premiums (both employee and employer parts)
  • Security tax (same deadline as income tax)
  • Funded pension contributions

6.2. Monthly Declaration (TSD)

By the same 10th day deadline, employers must submit the monthly TSD declaration, which now includes fields for reporting withheld security tax amounts.

6.3. What Employers Must Track

  • Employee's chosen funded pension rate (2%, 4%, or 6%) — check in e-MTA
  • Whether employee is exempt from funded pension
  • Employee's tax-exempt income status (regular vs. pensioner)
  • Correct application of the new 2% security tax to all taxable wages

🎯 The Bottom Line for 2026: Payroll calculation becomes slightly more complex due to the introduction of the 2% security tax, but the system remains transparent. The key changes are:

  1. Withhold an additional 2% security tax from all employee gross wages
  2. Apply the new fixed tax-exempt amounts (€700/€776)
  3. Continue using the same rates for income tax (22%), social tax (33%), and unemployment insurance

With correct calculations and timely declarations, you can avoid tax arrears, penalties, and ensure smooth operations with the Tax and Customs Board.
