AML Requirements for Estonian Companies: A Complete Guide for 2026

2026-03-13 Category: AML

Important: This article provides general information about AML requirements in Estonia based on the Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing Prevention Act (RahaPTS). It does not constitute legal advice. Each company's obligations may vary depending on its specific activities and risk profile. We recommend consulting with qualified legal and compliance professionals for advice tailored to your situation.

AML is not just a buzzword — it's a legal obligation

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations in Estonia are strict, comprehensive, and enforced with significant penalties. Ignoring these requirements can lead to fines of up to €5 million, criminal liability for directors, or even license revocation.

In this guide, we break down who is subject to the Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing Prevention Act (RahaPTS), what procedures must be implemented, and how to avoid critical mistakes in 2026.

2026 Update: Estonian AML requirements remain fully aligned with EU's 5th and 6th Anti-Money Laundering Directives. Key focus areas include beneficial ownership transparency, enhanced due diligence for high-risk countries, and strict rules for virtual asset service providers (VASPs).

1. Who is an "Obliged Entity"? (Who must comply?)

The AML law applies to a much wider range of businesses than most people think. According to § 2 of the Act, the list includes:

Financial Institutions

  • Credit institutions (banks)
  • Currency exchange offices
  • Payment and e-money institutions
  • Insurance companies and intermediaries
  • Investment firms and fund managers

Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs)

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges
  • Crypto wallet providers
  • ICO issuers and platforms

Professional Service Providers

  • Notaries, attorneys, bailiffs, bankruptcy trustees — when handling client funds or facilitating financial/real estate transactions
  • Auditors, accountants, tax advisors — when providing services listed in the Act
  • Trust and company service providers — formation agents, legal address providers, nominee directors

Non-Financial Sector (High-Value Dealers)

  • Cash traders: Anyone receiving cash payments of €10,000 or more (including linked transactions)
  • Real estate agents and rental intermediaries: For transactions or monthly rent ≥ €10,000
  • Precious metals/stones and art dealers: Transactions ≥ €10,000
  • Gambling operators: Casinos, online betting platforms

NGOs and Foundations

  • When receiving or making cash payments exceeding €5,000

⚠️ Warning: Even if your main business is not financial, a single cash transaction over €10,000 makes you an "obliged entity" for that transaction, with full reporting requirements.

2. The Core Principle: Know Your Customer (KYC)

At the heart of AML compliance is customer due diligence (CDD). You must know who your client is, understand their business, and verify their information.

When must you apply CDD measures? (§ 19)

  • When establishing a business relationship (opening an account, starting cooperation)
  • For occasional transactions ≥ €15,000 (or a series of linked transactions)
  • Whenever there is a suspicion of money laundering (regardless of amount)
  • When you doubt previously obtained data (e.g., client's information seems inconsistent)

What does CDD include? (§ 20)

The standard "KYC package" requires you to:

  1. Identify the client — Verify identity using a valid passport/ID card (in person or via secure remote identification as per § 31, such as Smart-ID or video identification).
  2. Verify representatives — Check their authority to act on behalf of the client.
  3. Identify the beneficial owner — Determine who ultimately owns or controls the entity (see section 3 below).
  4. Understand the business relationship — Know the purpose of the account, source of funds, and expected transaction patterns.
  5. Check PEP status — Determine if the client or their family members are Politically Exposed Persons (requires enhanced due diligence under § 41).
  6. Ongoing monitoring — Continuously review transactions to ensure they match the client's profile.

3. The Beneficial Owner: The Most Critical Detail

Requirements for identifying and registering beneficial owners are detailed in Chapter 9 of the Act. This is where many Estonian companies get into trouble.

Who is a beneficial owner? (§ 9)

A natural person who, directly or indirectly:

  • Owns more than 25% of shares or voting rights
  • Exercises control by other means (agreements, veto rights, etc.)
  • If no such person exists, senior management must be registered as "pseudo-beneficial owners"

Registration requirements (§ 77)

Data must be entered into the Beneficial Ownership Information Database (kasuliku tegeliku kasusaaja andmekogu) via the e-Business Register portal.

  • Deadline: Submit upon company registration and update within 30 days of any change
  • Verification: Notaries, banks, and other obliged entities must verify your data against the register
  • Public access: Information is accessible to authorities and obliged entities (limited public access with legitimate interest)

Penalties for non-compliance (§ 95)

  • For legal persons: Up to €400,000
  • For natural persons (directors): Fines and potential disqualification
  • Repeated violations: Can lead to forced dissolution of the company

✨ Pro tip: Check your company's data in the register today. Even if you submitted it years ago, any change in ownership structure must be updated within 30 days.

4. Internal Procedures: Your AML Shield

Identifying clients is not enough. You must have documented systems and trained staff. §§ 13 and 14 require:

Conduct a Risk Assessment

You must understand the risks inherent in your business model, client base, and geographic reach. Consider:

  • Client risk (high-risk industries, PEPs, complex structures)
  • Product/service risk (cash-intensive, cross-border, anonymous)
  • Geographic risk (countries with weaker AML regimes)

Develop Internal Rules (Protseduurireeglid)

This document must clearly outline:

  • How you identify clients and verify their identity
  • How you determine and document risk profiles
  • How you identify and report suspicious transactions
  • How you retain records (minimum 5 years as per § 51)
  • Roles and responsibilities of staff

Appoint a Responsible Person (§ 17)

High-risk obliged entities (banks, crypto firms, payment institutions) must designate a compliance officer responsible for:

  • Implementing AML procedures
  • Liaising with the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU)
  • Receiving and acting on internal suspicious transaction reports

Train Employees (§ 14(6))

Regular AML training is mandatory for all relevant staff. Training must cover:

  • Current AML laws and regulations
  • Internal procedures and reporting channels
  • How to recognize red flags
  • Consequences of non-compliance

5. Suspicious Transactions: When to Sound the Alarm?

§ 49 is the most critical provision for any obliged entity. If you notice something unusual, you must report it to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) immediately, but no later than within 2 working days.

What are "red flags"?

  • Complex, illogical transaction structures with no economic purpose
  • Client refuses to provide information about beneficial ownership
  • Transactions from accounts in high-risk countries (as listed by FATF or EU)
  • Urgent requests to transfer funds immediately after account opening
  • Transactions that are unusually large compared to normal activity
  • Client seems unconcerned about high fees or unfavorable terms
  • Use of cash when normal business practice would use banking channels

Reporting procedure

  1. Internal report: Employee reports suspicion to the designated responsible person
  2. Analysis: Responsible person evaluates if suspicion is founded
  3. External report: If confirmed, submit to FIU via their secure portal within 2 working days
  4. Tipping off prohibition: You CANNOT inform the client that a report has been made (§ 93)

Safe harbor: The law protects you if you report a suspicion in good faith, even if it turns out to be unfounded. You cannot be sued for breach of confidentiality.

6. Prohibition on Anonymity (§ 25)

The Act strictly prohibits anonymous accounts, passbooks, or crypto-wallets. Any business relationship must be personalized and traceable.

  • No numbered accounts: All accounts must be in the name of an identified person
  • No anonymous transactions: All transactions must be attributable to an identified client
  • No shell companies: Clients cannot use corporate vehicles to hide ownership without disclosure

7. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) — §§ 40-43

For higher-risk situations, standard CDD is not enough. You must apply enhanced measures:

When is EDD required?

  • PEPs and their family members (§ 41) — Establish source of wealth and funds
  • Cross-border correspondent relationships (§ 42) — For financial institutions
  • Complex or unusually large transactions
  • Clients from high-risk third countries (as listed by EU)
  • Whenever normal CDD raises suspicion

EDD measures include:

  • Additional information on the client's business and ownership
  • Source of wealth and source of funds documentation
  • Senior management approval to establish/continue relationship
  • Enhanced monitoring of transactions

8. Penalties for Violations (Chapter 10)

Estonian AML penalties are severe and actively enforced by the FIU and supervisory authorities.

For legal persons:

  • Up to €5 million OR up to 10% of annual global turnover (whichever is higher)
  • License suspension or revocation
  • Public naming and reputational damage
  • Forced dissolution in extreme cases

For natural persons (directors, responsible persons):

  • Large personal fines
  • Disqualification from serving as a director
  • Criminal liability for intentional violations (up to 3 years imprisonment)

Most common violations:

  • Failure to identify beneficial owner (up to €400,000 for companies)
  • Failure to report suspicious transactions
  • Inadequate internal procedures or training
  • Poor record-keeping

9. Exceptions for Legal Professionals (§ 45)

Attorneys, notaries, and auditors have specific exemptions when the information is protected by legal privilege.

When are they exempt?

  • When defending a client in court proceedings
  • When providing legal advice regarding ongoing or contemplated litigation

When are they NOT exempt?

  • When forming companies or providing trust services
  • When handling client funds
  • When acting as financial intermediaries
  • When suspicion arises outside privileged activities

10. Record-Keeping Requirements (§ 51)

All obliged entities must maintain records for at least 5 years after:

  • The end of a business relationship
  • The date of an occasional transaction

What must be kept?

  • Copies of identification documents
  • Transaction records
  • Risk assessments
  • Suspicious transaction reports (including internal analysis)
  • Training records

⚠️ After 5 years, data must be securely deleted, unless required for ongoing legal proceedings.

Summary: A Checklist for Company Directors

✅ Your AML To-Do List for 2026

  • Register beneficial owners — Check the e-Business Register today. Is your data correct and up-to-date?
  • Implement KYC procedures — Do you have a documented process for identifying new clients?
  • Draft internal rules — Create a written AML procedures document (required for all obliged entities).
  • Train your staff — Conduct AML training at least annually and keep records.
  • Know your red flags — Ensure your team can spot suspicious transactions.
  • Establish reporting lines — Know who to contact at the FIU and how.
  • Retain records — Set up a system to store AML documents for 5 years.
  • Review high-risk clients — Apply enhanced due diligence where needed.

Key Takeaways

  • AML applies to more businesses than you think — Even a single €10,000 cash transaction triggers obligations.
  • Know your beneficial owner — This is the single most common compliance failure.
  • Document everything — If it's not documented, it didn't happen.
  • Report suspicions — The FIU takes non-reporting very seriously.
  • Penalties are severe — Up to €5 million for companies.

🎯 The bottom line: AML compliance in Estonia is not optional — it's a legal requirement with real teeth. But with proper procedures, trained staff, and regular monitoring, compliance is manageable. The cost of getting it wrong (fines, reputational damage, criminal liability) far outweighs the investment in getting it right.
