VAT Rules for Small Businesses in Estonia in 2026

2026-03-13 Category: VAT

Important: This article provides general information about VAT rules applicable in 2026. Tax laws can be complex and every business situation is unique. The thresholds and rules described here are based on the Value-Added Tax Act as of 2026, but professional advice is recommended before making decisions that could affect your tax liability.

Short answer: VAT is simpler than it looks (once you know the rules)

Many entrepreneurs panic when they first hear about VAT. But the system is actually logical: you charge VAT on your sales, reclaim VAT on your purchases, and pay the difference to the tax authority. The tricky part? Knowing when you must register and when you can stay small. Let's break it down.

2026 update: The VAT landscape has become significantly more entrepreneur-friendly thanks to the EU-wide special procedure for small businesses. Cross-border selling is now much simpler, and the thresholds have been clarified. This article reflects all changes effective as of January 1, 2026.

1. Who must register? The general obligation

The basic rule is refreshingly simple:

If your taxable supply in Estonia exceeds €40,000 in a calendar year, you must register as a VAT taxable person.

Key details:

  • Calculation period: The threshold is calculated from the beginning of the current calendar year (January 1st). No more retroactive calculations based on previous years.
  • When it triggers: The obligation arises on the exact day your turnover exceeds €40,000, not at the end of the month or quarter.
  • Exception: If your entire turnover consists of exempt supplies (like health services) or 0% rate supplies (like exports), you don't need to register — even if you exceed €40,000.

Example: An IT consultant starts in January 2026. In September, their invoices total €42,000. They must register immediately and start charging VAT from that day. Before September, all invoices were VAT-free.

2. Game-changer: Special procedure for small businesses (VAT Act § 19¹)

Since January 1, 2025, Estonian small businesses have access to a powerful EU-wide simplification. This is perhaps the most important change for small exporters.

The problem it solves:

Previously, if you sold goods to consumers in Germany, you had to either register for VAT in Germany (costly and bureaucratic) or use the One Stop Shop (OSS) system and charge German VAT from Estonia. Both added complexity.

The solution:

The special procedure for small businesses lets you apply for an "EX" suffix registration number from the Estonian Tax and Customs Board. This number tells other EU countries: "This business is exempt from registering here because it's small in its home country."

Conditions (must meet BOTH):

  1. EU-wide supply under €100,000: Your total supply across the entire European Union (including Estonia) must not exceed €100,000 in the current or previous calendar year.
  2. Destination country threshold: Your supply in that specific member state must not exceed that country's own small business threshold. These vary by country (e.g., Germany €100,000, France €85,800, etc.).

Example: An Estonian craft seller has total EU sales of €80,000, with €15,000 going to Germany. Germany's threshold is €100,000. They can apply for the EX number and sell to German consumers without registering in Germany or charging German VAT.

✨ This means you can sell across the EU with zero additional VAT bureaucracy — as long as you stay under the limits!

3. The €10,000 threshold: Digital services and distance selling

Even if you don't use the special procedure above, another important threshold applies specifically to cross-border B2C sales.

What it covers:

  • Intra-Community distance selling — goods sold to consumers in other EU countries
  • Electronically supplied services — digital products, apps, e-books, streaming, online courses
  • Telecom and broadcasting services

The rule:

  • Below €10,000/year total to all EU countries: You can keep charging Estonian VAT at Estonian rates. Simple.
  • Above €10,000/year: The destination principle kicks in. You must charge VAT at the rate of the customer's country.

But don't panic — this is where the One Stop Shop (OSS) comes in. Instead of registering in every country, you declare everything through Estonia's OSS portal once per quarter. One declaration, one payment, one place.

Important: The €10,000 threshold applies per business, not per country. Once you cross it, all your cross-border B2C digital sales become subject to destination country rates — not just the amount above the threshold.

4. The reverse charge: Taxable person with limited liability (VAT Act § 21)

As a small business, you might not be a "regular" taxable person, but you could become a taxable person with limited liability. This sounds scary, but it's actually a common situation.

When it happens:

You must register as a taxable person with limited liability if:

  • You receive services from abroad (consulting, advertising, software, legal advice) from a foreign provider not registered in Estonia, and those services are taxable here.
  • Your intra-Community acquisitions of goods exceed €10,000 in a calendar year.

What it means:

You become responsible for calculating and paying the VAT on those purchases yourself. This is called the reverse charge. You don't actually pay money to the foreign supplier — instead, you declare the VAT in your own return and simultaneously reclaim it (if you have the right to deduct). Net effect is often zero, but the paperwork must be done.

Example: A small Estonian marketing agency buys €5,000 worth of software from a US company. The US company doesn't charge VAT. The agency must register as a limited liability taxable person, declare €1,200 VAT (24%) on this purchase, and can immediately deduct it (if used for business). Net cost to the agency: still €5,000.

5. VAT rates in 2026

Here are the rates you'll actually charge (if registered):

RateApplies to
24%Standard rate — most goods and services
13%Accommodation services (hotels, hostels, short-term rentals)
9%Books, educational literature, press publications (print and digital), certain medicinal products and sanitary items
0%Exports, intra-Community supplies, international transport

Supply exempt from tax (no VAT charged, but also no input VAT deduction):

  • Health services
  • Insurance services
  • Leasing or letting of immovable property (except short-term accommodation and parking)
  • Financial services

⚠️ Critical point: If you make exempt supplies, you generally cannot deduct input VAT on related purchases. This is a major consideration for businesses like healthcare providers or landlords.

6. Accounting simplifications for small businesses

VAT isn't the only thing simplified for small businesses. The Accounting Act offers significant relief based on size.

Micro-undertaking (must meet at least two):

  • Total assets < €450,000
  • Revenue < €900,000
  • Average employees < 10

Simplification: May prepare an abridged annual report — balance sheet and profit and loss account only. No cash flow statement, no equity statement required.

Small undertaking (must meet at least two):

  • Total assets < €7.5 million
  • Revenue < €15 million
  • Average employees < 50

Simplification: Also eligible for the abridged annual report. Complex reporting is optional.

Bottom line: Unless you're a medium or large business, your statutory reporting can be refreshingly simple. Focus on running your business, not preparing complicated financial statements.

7. Practical summary: A checklist for small business owners

Before you start selling:

  • Estimate your annual turnover. Will it exceed €40,000? If yes, plan to register.
  • Check your EU sales. Will you sell to consumers in other EU countries? If yes, consider the special procedure for small businesses.
  • Review your purchases from abroad. Are you buying services from non-Estonian providers? You may need to register as a limited liability person.

If you're already selling:

  • Monitor your turnover monthly. The €40,000 threshold triggers on the day it's exceeded.
  • Track your EU sales separately. Know when you approach the €10,000 threshold for digital services/distance selling.
  • Consider your company size. Are you a micro or small undertaking? You may qualify for simplified accounting.

8. Common pitfalls to avoid

🚫 Registering too late

The obligation triggers on the day turnover exceeds €40,000. If you register a month later, you owe VAT on all invoices from that day — and may face penalties.

🚫 Ignoring the reverse charge

Buying services from abroad without registering as a limited liability person is technically tax evasion. The foreign supplier won't charge VAT, but you're supposed to declare it. Most small businesses don't realize this.

🚫 Confusing exempt with 0%

Exempt means no VAT charged AND no input VAT recovery. 0% means no VAT charged BUT you can still reclaim VAT on related costs. Different rules, big difference.

🚫 Missing the €10,000 threshold

Once you cross it, all your cross-border B2C digital sales become taxable in the customer's country — not just the excess. This catches many businesses off guard.

Summary: Your VAT decision tree for 2026

  1. Turnover in Estonia > €40,000? → Must register as taxable person.
  2. Selling to EU consumers? → Consider the special procedure for small businesses (EX number).
  3. Selling digital services to EU consumers > €10,000? → Use One Stop Shop (OSS) to charge destination country VAT.
  4. Buying services from abroad? → Check if you need to register as limited liability person and apply reverse charge.
  5. Small company? → Enjoy simplified accounting — abridged annual report only.

🎯 The bottom line: VAT in Estonia is logical and increasingly entrepreneur-friendly. The 2025/2026 changes — especially the special procedure for small businesses — have removed many of the barriers to cross-border trade. The key is knowing your thresholds, monitoring them regularly, and getting advice when you're unsure.
