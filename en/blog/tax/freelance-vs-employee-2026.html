Freelance vs Employment in Estonia: Which Pays Off in 2026?

2026-03-13 Category: Taxes

Important: This article is for informational purposes and describes legal tax optimization methods provided by Estonian legislation. Paying minimum wage must correspond to actual work activities. Artificially lowering your salary without economic justification is unacceptable. Every situation is unique — we recommend consulting our specialists to calculate the optimal strategy for your specific case.

The short answer: it's more complicated than it seems

Many people think employment is better because "someone else pays your taxes". In reality, your employer pays taxes from their own pocket, not from your salary. In 2026, there's also a new security tax (2%) that needs to be factored in. Let's break it down with real numbers.

Note: Estonian legislation gives entrepreneurs the choice between different business forms and income strategies. For example, registering as an FIE (sole proprietor), you can pay social tax only on the minimum wage — this is explicitly allowed by law. The rest of your income is only subject to income tax and security tax. However, your actual activities must match your chosen model — our accountants can help you find the right balance between optimization and compliance.

Comparison: €3,000 monthly income

Scenario 1: Employee

  • Gross salary: €3,000
  • Taxes:
    • Income tax: €506 ((3,000-700)×22%)
    • Unemployment (1.6%): €48
    • Pension (2%): €60
    • Security tax (2%): €60
  • Net take-home: €2,326
  • Actual employer cost: €4,014 (€3,000 + €990 social tax + €24 unemployment)

Scenario 2: Freelancer (worst case - no optimization)

  • Income: €3,000
  • Taxes:
    • Income tax: €506
    • Social tax (33%): €990
    • Security tax (2%): €60
    • Pension (2%, optional): €60
  • Net take-home: €1,384 (€1,444 without pension)

⚠️ This is definitely not the way to pay taxes!

Scenario 3: Freelancer as FIE (sole proprietor - optimized)

Register as an FIE (sole proprietor) and pay social tax only on the minimum wage (in 2026 minimum wage is €886):

  • Income: €3,000
  • Social tax (on €886): €292.38
  • Income tax: €506
  • Security tax (2%): €60
  • Net take-home: €2,141.62

✨ Already close to the employee's €2,326, but with entrepreneur benefits!

Scenario 4: Freelancer via OÜ (Ltd) - optimal for higher incomes

Once your income exceeds €25,000-30,000/year, it's better to open a company:

  • Revenue: €3,000
  • Pay yourself minimum wage (€886):
    • Income tax: €41 ((886-700)×22%)
    • Unemployment: €14.18
    • Pension: €17.72
    • Security tax: €17.72
    • Net from salary: €795.38
  • Left in company: €2,114
  • Corporate income tax when withdrawing as dividends (22/78): €465
  • Net dividends: €1,649
  • Total take-home: €2,444

✨ That's €118 more than the employee!

Step-by-step strategy for freelancers

Step 1: Start with FIE (income up to €2,000-2,500/month)

  • Register as an FIE (simple and cheap)
  • Pay social tax only on the minimum wage (€886)
  • The rest is business income

Result: With €2,500 income, take-home ~€1,850

Step 2: Switch to OÜ (stable income from €2,000-2,500/month)

  • Open an OÜ (a bit more complex, but more profitable)
  • Pay yourself minimum wage (€886)
  • Take the rest as dividends quarterly/yearly

Result: With €3,000 income, take-home ~€2,444

Step 3: Mixed strategy (advanced level)

  • Pay yourself an optimal salary (not necessarily minimum)
  • Leave some profit in the company for investments
  • Buy assets through the company (laptop, car, tools)
  • Use business travel and representation expenses

Key 2026 nuances

  • Security tax (2%): New tax that must be included in all calculations. For employees, it's withheld from salary; for freelancers, you pay it yourself.
  • Tax-free allowance: From 2026, it's fixed at €700 for everyone (except pensioners who get €776). No more complicated formulas!
  • Sick leave: Freelancers don't get it (but you can buy insurance)
  • Vacation: You plan and save for it yourself
  • Pension: Social tax on minimum wage gives you pension qualifying years (important for your future state pension)
  • Expenses: Freelancers can deduct equipment, internet, training, and part of utilities

2026 conclusions

Freelancing is better if:

  • Your income exceeds €25,000-30,000/year
  • You're willing to handle optimization (or delegate it to an accountant)
  • You have business expenses to deduct
  • You value flexibility and can handle income instability

Employment is better if:

  • You value stability, paid sick leave, and vacation
  • You don't want to deal with paperwork and reporting
  • Your income is under €15,000-18,000/year
  • Social protection is important to you

🎯 2026 Bottom line: With an annual income of €36,000, a freelancer via OÜ takes home about €29,328, while an employee with the same cost to the employer takes home about €27,912. That's a €1,416 per year advantage for freelancing. And with expense optimization, the gap widens even further.

But the real advantage of freelancing is scalability. When your income grows to €60,000/year, the difference becomes €5,000+ per year.
